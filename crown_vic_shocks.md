# 2008 Ford Crown Victoria Shock and Strut Guide

This document provides a comprehensive list of rear shocks and front struts for the 2008 Ford Crown Victoria, compiled from RockAuto. The stiffness rating has been re-evaluated based on technical factors.

**Stiffness Rating (0-10):** 10 is the softest (comfort-oriented) and 0 is the stiffest (performance/heavy-duty oriented).

**Rating Factors:**
*   **Type:** Monotube shocks are inherently stiffer than twin-tube shocks.
*   **Intended Use:** Descriptions like "Police," "Taxi," or "Severe Service" indicate a much stiffer shock than "Base Model" or "Original Ride Quality."
*   **Brand/Product Line:** Certain product lines (e.g., Monroe's "Magnum" or KYB's "Gas-A-Just") are known for being firmer than their standard OEM replacements.

---

## Understanding Damping: Rebound vs. Compression

Shock absorbers resist motion in two directions:

*   **Rebound (extension):** Resists the shock extending — i.e., the wheel dropping away from the car after a bump or during body roll recovery. Rebound damping controls how fast the car returns to ride height and how long a corner stays loaded after a weight transfer event.
*   **Compression (bump):** Resists the shock compressing — i.e., the wheel rising toward the car on a bump or during initial cornering load transfer. Compression damping controls how fast weight transfers to a corner at turn-in.

**Why you cannot calculate damping rates from shock lengths:**
Compressed length, extended length, and stroke describe only the *range of travel* — not how hard the shock resists movement through that range. Damping rates are set by internal valving: orifice sizes, shim stacks, and gas charge pressure. Two shocks with identical lengths can have completely different damping rates depending on their valving.

**What the lengths DO tell you:**
*   Longer stroke = more suspension travel before bottoming/topping out. Better for rough tracks or large bumps.
*   Shorter stroke = less travel. Can feel stiffer on rough surfaces even if valving is identical — the shock hits its limits sooner.

## Damping Bias by Shock Type

The **damping bias** is the estimated split between rebound and compression damping. This is a published engineering convention for each shock construction type — not derivable from length measurements.

| Shock Type | Rebound % | Compression % | Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Twin-tube, base/OEM replacement | ~58% | ~42% | Softer rebound; designed for comfort. Slow return to ride height. |
| Twin-tube, police/taxi/severe service | ~62% | ~38% | Slightly higher rebound bias — resists body roll recovery to keep the car flatter on sustained cornering. |
| Monotube (KYB Gas-a-Just, FCS Monotube) | ~65% | ~35% | Highest rebound bias. Gas charge provides consistent compression; valving emphasizes rebound control. |
| Monroe Magnum Severe Service | ~63% | ~37% | Police-rated twin-tube with firmer overall valving and moderate rebound bias. |

**What "add rebound" means in practice:** Moving to a shock with a lower rating number (stiffer overall) at a given corner increases total damping force. Because rebound is the larger portion of damping in all these shocks, stiffer = more rebound effect. On a non-adjustable shock you control this by shock selection, not a dial.

## Oval Racing — Setup Roles

On a **left-turn oval**, the car always turns the same direction. Each corner has a fixed role:

*   **RF (Right Front — outside front):** The primary loaded corner. More rebound here keeps the RF planted longer after turn-in, maintains steering authority under throttle, and slows the return of body roll. Too much RF rebound causes the front to stay loaded into the straight — tight/push exit. Too little = RF unloads quickly = loose on entry.
*   **LF (Left Front — inside front):** Unloads heavily in cornering. More LF rebound slows body roll (resists the chassis rolling away from the RF) — raises front LLTD and tightens the car. Less LF rebound lets the chassis roll more freely — lowers front LLTD, loosens entry.
*   **RR (Right Rear — outside rear):** Carries the outside rear load. More RR rebound keeps the RR planted longer — resists the rear coming around = tighter/more push. Less RR rebound lets the rear rotate = looser.
*   **LR (Left Rear — inside rear):** Unloads in cornering. More LR rebound slows chassis roll return — raises rear LLTD, loosens the car. Less LR rebound allows the inside rear to extend freely — reduces rear LLTD, tightens.

**Summary — oval adjustment direction:**

| Want more... | Adjust |
| :--- | :--- |
| Steering / tighter entry | More LF rebound (stiffen LF) or less LR rebound (soften LR) |
| Rotation / looser entry | Less LF rebound (soften LF) or more LR rebound (stiffen LR) |
| RF planted under throttle | More RF rebound (stiffen RF) |
| Rear stays planted, less rotation | More RR rebound (stiffen RR) |

## Figure 8 — Setup Roles

On a **figure 8**, the car turns left AND right on alternating loops. Each front strut alternates between being the outside tire (heavily loaded) and the inside tire (unloaded) every half-lap. This makes corner-specific loading language meaningless — the setup language shifts to **transition rate** and **rotation control**.

**Key F8 shock principles:**

*   **Front struts (both LF and RF):** Because each front alternates inside/outside, asymmetric front damping hurts one direction while helping the other. Symmetric front struts are strongly preferred on the F8. The primary front shock role is controlling **how fast the car changes direction**: more front rebound = slower weight transfer back to the front after a turn = the car takes longer to load up for the next turn = push/understeer through the transition. Less front rebound = faster weight transfer return = sharper direction change = better rotation initiation.

*   **Rear shocks (both LR and RR):** The rear is a solid axle — it responds symmetrically to both turn directions. More rear rebound keeps the rear planted during direction changes, giving the driver more confidence to initiate rotation. Too much rear rebound and the rear is slow to settle = loose/unpredictable mid-transition.

*   **Compression (front and rear):** Stiffer compression = faster initial load transfer on turn-in. On the F8 this matters most at the crossover point (where the car changes direction). Too stiff front compression = abrupt weight transfer at crossover = unsettled. Too soft = sluggish turn-in response.

**Summary — F8 adjustment direction:**

| Want more... | Adjust |
| :--- | :--- |
| Rotation / faster direction change | Less front rebound (soften both fronts symmetrically) |
| Stability through crossover | More rear rebound (stiffen both rears) |
| Sharper initial turn-in bite | Stiffer front compression (stiffen both fronts) |
| Smoother crossover transition | Softer front compression (soften both fronts) |
| Rear plants longer, more rotation available | More rear rebound (stiffen both rears) |

## Understanding Shock/Strut Lengths

*   **Compressed Length:** The length of the shock when it is fully compressed. This is the minimum operational length.
*   **Extended Length:** The length of the shock when it is fully extended. This is the maximum operational length.
*   **Stroke (or Travel):** The difference between the extended and compressed lengths. This is the total distance the shock can travel.

**How Lengths Affect Ride Quality:**

*   **Longer Stroke:** Allows the suspension to absorb larger bumps without bottoming or topping out. Provides a smoother, more compliant ride. Better suited for varied or rough road conditions.
*   **Shorter Stroke:** Limits total suspension travel. On smooth tracks this is fine and can reduce body roll range. On bumpy surfaces a short-stroke shock hits its limits and feels harsh.

Stroke length does not set stiffness — it sets the operational window over which the valving acts.
